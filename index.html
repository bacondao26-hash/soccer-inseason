Why This Exists
The case for in-season S&C
Most athletes stop lifting in-season. The result: 10–20% strength loss in 6–8 weeks. Strength loss means slower acceleration, lower jump height, and significantly higher injury risk — especially hamstring strains — in the second half of the season. That's when most season-ending injuries happen.
Two sessions per week is all you need. The goal is not to get fitter. It's to maintain what you built and stay healthy for 90 minutes in game 30 the same way you were in game 1.
Less volume, same intensity. In-season S&C is not watered-down off-season training — it's a different tool. The loads stay high so your muscles stay adapted. The volume drops so your legs are ready for games.
Setup
How to schedule your week
With 2 games per week, the training window is tight. Two rules that override everything else: no strength work within 24 hours of a game. No speed work within 48 hours of a game.
Template below uses Wed/Sun as game days. Adjust to your schedule — the spacing principle stays the same.

Session A
Strength + Injury Prevention
~50 min  ·  2–3 days before next game
Compound Strength

Goal: Keep the strength you built in pre-season. Keep the hamstrings and adductors loaded so they stay resilient all season. Short, sharp, no junk volume.
Warm-Up — 8 min
5 min jog or bike → Leg swings front/back × 10 each → Leg swings side/side × 10 each → Bodyweight squat × 10 → Hip hinge × 10 → Lateral band walk × 10 each direction. Done.

Trap Bar Deadlift
Primary strength movement for the session. Feet hip-width inside the bar. Push the floor away — don't think about pulling. Stand tall at the top. Lower with control. This is the best single exercise for in-season lower body maintenance.

Rest 2 min

Back foot on bench, dumbbells in hand. Drop straight down — don't lean forward. Two sets only — volume is intentionally low for an in-season body. Execution quality over load.

Rest 90s

Block C — Injury Prevention
Non-Negotiable — Do not cut this block
Nordic curls and Copenhagen planks reduce hamstring and groin injury risk by up to 50%. A pulled hamstring ends your season. Ten minutes of prevention does not.
C1
Nordic Hamstring Curl
Kneel, feet held under a fixed bar or by a partner. Lower toward the floor as slowly as possible — 4 full seconds down. Catch with hands. Assist back up. The lowering phase is the training stimulus. Rushing through this defeats the purpose.
3 × 5
4s eccentric
Rest 90s
C2
Copenhagen Plank
Side plank with top foot or knee on bench. Hold both sides every session. Adductor loading — the most evidence-backed groin injury prevention exercise in soccer. Groin strains are the most common non-contact injury in the sport. This is your prevention protocol.
3 × 20s each
Rest 45s
Block D — Core
D1
Dead Bug
Flat on back, lower back pressed to floor. Opposite arm and leg lower slowly while breathing out. Back stays flat throughout. Spinal stability — the foundation of every athletic movement.
3 × 8 each
Rest 45s
D2
Pallof Press
Cable or band anchored to side. Press straight out from chest, hold 2 seconds, return. Body resists rotation throughout. Anti-rotation core training — directly specific to cutting, shielding, and change of direction.
3 × 10 each
Rest 45s
Session B
Speed + Power
~40 min  ·  2 days before next game
Max Sprint
Box Jump
Broad Jump
Med Ball
Goal: Maintain sprint speed and explosive power through the full season. Low volume, maximum quality. Every rep at 95%+ effort. This is the session most athletes skip in-season. That's why they're slower in November than September.
Sprint Warm-Up — 10 min (Required)
2 min jog → High knees 2×20yd → Butt kicks 2×20yd → A-march 2×20yd → Leg swings both planes → 4 build-up sprints: 40yd starting at 60%, finishing at 90%. Walk back after each. Cold muscles and sprinting is how hamstrings get pulled.
Sprint Block
Rule: Full recovery between every rep — no exceptions
If you're not fully recovered, wait longer. Fatigued sprints train bad mechanics and accomplish nothing. Quality only.
S1
10-Yard Sprint
Standing start. Aggressive forward lean, explosive first step. Max intent every rep. Walk back, wait until fully ready, go again.
4–6 reps
Walk-back recovery
S2
20-Yard Sprint
Drive the first 10, let your stride open the back 10. Relax your hands, jaw, and face. Tension is the enemy of speed.
4 reps
Walk-back + 30s
S3
40-Yard Sprint
Max-velocity exposure. Two reps, 3-minute full rest between each. These are the highest neurological demand reps of your week. Never rush the rest.
2 reps
3 min rest each
Power Block — Full rest between sets
P1
Box Jump
Max effort every rep. Full arm swing, full body extension at takeoff. Land in a quarter-squat on the box. Step down. 90-second rest between sets — this is neuromuscular work, not cardio.
3 × 4
90s between sets
P2
Broad Jump
Max distance every rep. Land in an athletic position and absorb the landing — soft knees. The landing quality matters: good deceleration mechanics protect your knees in-season.
3 × 3
90s between sets
P3
Med Ball Rotational Throw
Side-on to a concrete wall, 3–4 feet away. Load the back hip, rotate explosively from the hips, throw hard into the wall. Hip rotation power — specific to shooting, turning under pressure, and winning physical duels.
3 × 4 each side
60s between sets
Cool-Down — 5 min
90/90 hip stretch × 60s each side → Standing quad stretch × 30s each → Hip flexor stretch × 30s each → Walk 2 min. Done.

Non-Negotiables
In-season rules
  • Session B before Session A in the training week. Speed needs a fresher nervous system than strength. If you can only do one session this week, do Session B.
  • Games take priority. If you're exhausted 48 hours before a game, skip or shorten the session. Arriving fresh is always the right call.
  • Never cut the prehab block. Nordic curls and Copenhagen planks in Block C are the most important exercises in this program. A hamstring strain ends your season.
  • Sprint quality over sprint quantity. Five perfect 10-yard sprints at 100% outvalue ten tired ones at 70%. Wait for full recovery between reps.
  • Sleep is your number one recovery tool. Two games and two sessions per week is a high training load. 8–9 hours is not a luxury — it is part of the program.
  • Match weeks are high-calorie weeks. Cutting carbs before games and skimping on protein after sessions is the fastest way to feel flat by the second half of the season.
Know the Difference
When to stop and get help
Muscle soreness is normal and expected. These are not:
  • Sharp pain in the back of the thigh during sprints or Nordic curls — stop immediately. Possible hamstring strain.
  • Groin pain during Copenhagen planks or kicking — reduce load and get it assessed before the next session.
  • Knee pain that worsens during squatting — stop that exercise. Do not push through progressive joint pain.
  • Pain that gets worse rep by rep — rack the weight and end that exercise for the session.
